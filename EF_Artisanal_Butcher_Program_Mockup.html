Lesson 1 · The Speed Rack Rule

Cross-contamination prevention starts with gravity: anything that can drip must never be above something it could contaminate. Learn this stacking order until it's automatic.

Top shelfCooked / Ready-to-Eat Seafood
MiddleRed Meat (Beef · Pork · Lamb · Bison)
Always bottomChicken & Poultry — NEVER above red meat
Meat and seafood are NEVER stacked on the same speed rack — designate and clearly mark racks by department. Shellfish always stacks below all other seafood.

Lesson 2 · Gloves: Two Triggers, Zero Exceptions

There are exactly two events that require a glove change — and they're separate triggers. If either one happens, the gloves come off.

Trigger 1 — Species: Change gloves every time you switch between species. Beef to chicken? Change. Pork to fish? Change.
Trigger 2 — Customer: Change gloves between every customer interaction when handling product.
⊕ Added Best Practice — Wash first: Gloves are not a substitute for clean hands. Wash hands before gloving up at the start of a task, after breaks, and any time gloves come off. Gloves over dirty hands just contaminate slower.⊕ Added
Articulate: Interactive slider or process block

Lesson 3 · Sanitizer: 200–400ppm, Every 2 Hours

Sanitizer solution protects customers — but only at the right concentration. Too weak, it doesn't kill pathogens. Too strong, it contaminates food-contact surfaces.

Standard: Mix to 200–400ppm. Verify with test strips — never eyeball it.
Standard: Change sanitizer buckets every 2 hours during continuous use.
Standard: Spray-rag method only. NEVER spray a surface directly — spray the rag, then clean and sanitize the surface. This prevents aerosolizing chicken residue (salmonella risk).

Lesson 4 · Signage & COOL Labeling

Always
  • Clear P-Touch tape only
  • Price in LARGE P-Touch; COOL in SMALL
  • Unit of measure in caps, no period (LB, EA)
  • 4" holder front of case · 3" holder back of pans
  • COOL verified against the invoice daily
  • Sale signs checked daily — on-sale signed, off-sale removed
Never
  • White tape — ever
  • $ symbol on case signs
  • Chipped, crooked, or dirty signs
  • COOL missing on raw fresh/frozen product
  • Guessing origin — it comes from the invoice

COOL (Country of Origin Labeling) is required for all raw fresh or previously frozen fish/shellfish and applicable meat — but NOT for processed items (cooked, cured, smoked, restructured, or combined foods like Tilapia Parmesan). That distinction is the most common confusion point — build a true/false drill on it.

Lesson 2 · The Grind Day, Hour by Hour

Before anything

Review the Weekly Steak & Grind Report. Split the day's expected movement into thirds — that's your batch size. Grind pans do NOT need to be filled every morning.

Never before 8:30AM

Ground beef is the LAST item produced when filling the fresh case. Grinding early wastes peak-freshness display time.

10:00AM

Ground beef readiness standard — case set and full.

Through the day (min. 3x)

Grind fresh multiple times — minimum 3 grinds per day. Run out between 3–5PM? Grind another batch, and keep going as needed until 7PM. Log each batch in FRESH TRAX immediately.

After 7:00PM

Small loaves only — no more than 5 lbs at a time. Shift pans through the evening. Use roast or stew beef for PM grinds unless trimmings are on hand.

After 9:00PM

Clean the grinder — waiting until after 9PM allows for late grind special orders. Full nightly breakdown (covered in M3).

Standard: Fill grind pans LOOSELY — a lighter pack keeps grind red longer. Do not over-grind, and never produce backup grinds.

Lesson 3 · The Non-Negotiables

Always
  • Same-day grind — follow the Grind 101 SOP for ALL proteins
  • Correct fat content per the primal

Lesson 4 · Day-Old Ground Beef Protocol

By 8:00AM

Day-old grind is OUT of the meat case — no exceptions. It transfers to Prepared Foods through the register using the transfer procedure.

Pushing product to Prepared Foods?

Use the "PUSH" scan card at 40% cost.

Prepared Foods requests product (no leftover)?

Transfer at 100% cost.

Remember: 40% = you're moving YOUR leftover. 100% = they need FRESH product. The cost method tells the story of who initiated the transfer.

Lesson 1 · Hobart Band Saw — The Glove Rule Is Backwards Here

Always
  • All guards and covers properly fastened and tightened
  • Meat push plate used on EVERY cut
  • Cleaned thoroughly between each species
  • Broken down completely nightly — degreaser + J512 sanitizer
  • Left broken down to air dry overnight
  • Mineral oil on inner parts at reassembly
Never
  • NEVER wear a metal cutting glove while operating the saw
  • Never put hands near the blade — push plate only
  • Never skip between-species cleaning
  • Never reassemble wet
Why no metal glove? On a knife, a cut glove protects you. On a band saw, the blade can catch the metal mesh and pull your hand in. It's the opposite of knife intuition — which is exactly why we drill it.

Articulate: Process flow — animate left to right

Lesson 1 · The Set-Up Sequence Is a Food Safety Control

1
Cooked Section
Ready-to-eat set FIRST. Thawed, in shelf life, freshest to front.
✚
Sanitize
ALL working areas and tools cleaned AND sanitized — not just rinsed.
2
Fresh Fish
Black steak paper in every pan. Freshest to front. Max 2 layers.
3
Shellfish
Set LAST. Divider between fish and shellfish. Own pan or bowl each.
Why this order? Ready-to-eat food can never be at risk from raw product. Working cooked-first means raw juices never touch a surface that RTE food will touch. Skipping the sanitize step between sections is the single biggest food safety risk in seafood — and it also protects customers with shellfish allergies from cross-contact.

Lesson 2 · Three Ways to Spot Bad Seafood

1 · Smell: Fresh fish must not have a strong odor. Know your species baselines — cod and grouper have a distinct smell even when perfectly fresh.
2 · Texture: Flesh must be firm and intact. Soft, mushy, or broken fillets are signs of age.
3 · Color: Fish changes color as it ages — yellowing edges and darkening bloodlines mean the product has begun to turn.

Seafood Excellence II: Shellfish & Compliance

Shellfish carries our highest regulatory stakes: dig tags trace product in the event of illness, and the health department expects 100% compliance. This course also covers icing, the cooked section, and grab & go shelf life.

⏱ 15 minutes👥 Seafood Lead · APTL · TL⚠ Regulatory — 100% on compliance items

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  • Set and maintain the shellfish section: bluffing, poly bags, drain holes, re-icing

Articulate: Timeline interaction — the life of a dig tag

Lesson 1 · Dig Tags: The Life of a Tag

Delivery arrives

Verify EVERY container of live shellfish has a dig tag attached. Missing tag? Flag to your Team Leader immediately — before accepting.

While product sells

The tag stays ON the container until every shellfish from that container is sold or discarded. Never remove it because it "looks messy."

Last one sold

Write the date (month/day/year) on the tag in permanent marker — the LAST-SOLD date, not the received date. This starts the retention clock.

File it

Chronological order in the designated binder. Inspectors read disorganization as noncompliance.

90 days minimum

Tags retained at least 90 days from last-sold date and immediately retrievable for any health inspection.

Why it matters: Dig tags exist to trace shellfish in the event of a foodborne illness outbreak. This is the one process where "most of the time" equals failure — we are 100% compliant, every tag, every day.
Partner requirement: The raw shellfish consumer advisory sign is posted and visible in the department at ALL times. If it comes down for cleaning, it goes right back up.

Lesson 2 · Bluffing: Icing Like a Fishmonger

Always
  • Shellfish completely thawed before display
  • Bowls "bluffed" up with ice — full and overflowing look
  • Scallops and P&D shrimp in a clear poly bag — never touching ice
  • Drain holes in every bowl's corners
  • Mussels iced through the day to stay moist and closed

Never

  • Scallops or P&D shrimp in direct ice contact
  • Product sitting in standing water
  • Ice-once-in-the-morning-and-walk-away

All day
